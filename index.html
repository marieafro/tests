"Letterpress"

A printing technique that can be compared to stamping. The pressure with which the ink hits the paper creates greasy, not sharply cut edges on the sides of the image, which is not always clearly visible even with a magnifying glass.

"Fluorescent ink"

Ink that glows under ultraviolet light. The staff is a clear example.

"Fugitive ink"

An image that is applied with a stamp with ink. The size is virtually unlimited.

"Perforated"

An image (in most cases a number) that is punched into the passport.

"Gridded offset"

Image that is made up of very small dots or lines. Especially with the microscope well to be observed. The page cipher on this image is raster offset.

"Iris druk"

Print where one color flows smoothly into the other.

"Typescript"

Text typed with a typewriter or printer. With modern techniques it is often difficult to see the difference between the two.

"Microprinting"

Extremely small prints of composed lines or figures. The letters can only be distinguished through a magnifying glass.

"Offset"

Unlike book printing, the image is more even with offset. Also viewed under the microscope, it looks tight, without pressure edges. The difference between offset and book printing is also difficult to determine with current printing techniques. The silhouette in the middle of the image is a good example of offset.

"Plate printing"

Print where the ink lies on top of the paper to feel good.

"Embossing"

Pressure where, as it were, a must is pressed into the paper. Compare to a dry stamp, but applied by machine and not by hand. Larger formats are therefore possible.

"Simultaneous Pressure"

An image that is applied with a stamp with ink. The size is virtually unlimited.

"Screenprinting"

Pressure that is created because the ink has been pressed through a sieve, as it were. The image and the letters are therefore made up of dots (when viewed through a magnifying glass).

"Drying Stamp"

An image that is manually printed onto the paper or into the photo without ink with great force. The 'blemishes' that form that image can not only be seen, but can be felt even better.

"Ink Stamp"

An image that is applied with a stamp with ink. The size is virtually unlimited.

"Cahier stitch"

A stitch that holds the pages of the passport together in large loops. The thread can be seen in the center of the two center pages of the passport.

"Sewn on the flat"

Sewn some distance from the back. The thread is therefore visible on all pages and the booklet has a tendency to close again on its own.

"Sewn on the back"

In the spine of the passport, the thread with which the passport is bound can be seen in small stitches.

"Contrastrip"

To prevent a photo or text from being removed, a foil (plastic layer) is sometimes applied to a page. To hold the foil, the foil is sewn and folded. This creates a narrow 'contrast strip' of the same foil on the other side of the binding.

"Watermark"

Image placed in the paper during manufacture and which becomes clearly visible when the paper is held up to the light.

"Shadow Watermark"

A watermark with thick, gray lines.

"Two tone line watermark"

A watermark with both thick and thin linen. Here you can clearly see the rosettes.

"Line watermark"

A watermark composed of thin linen.

"Geometrical Figures"

Always repetitive shapes.

"Positioned image"

Image placed in exactly the same place on every page, as shown here. A non-positioned image is in a different place.

"Guilloche"

A page decoration of interwoven lines found in many passports.

"Lettering"

A continuous, repeating text that runs across the entire page. It is often recessed into the background printing.

"Logo"

Your own brand, a recognizable image. The cross of Switzerland is such a recognizable image.

"Underground printing" "Imprint"

A background print applied in soft tones, often leuering or geometric figures, over which the actual text to be read is printed. Texts and/or figures that are clearly printed over the background printing.

"Holographic plane"

An in itself flat image that nevertheless seems to have depth. This suggestion of depth only arises when one moves the passport.

"Latent image"

Image that can only really be seen properly when the passport is held up to the light at a certain angle. A letter appears in each link of the vertical decorative edge, together forming the word URUGUAY.

"Retroflecting image"

Image that only becomes visible when using a so-called retroviewer, a special lamp with a strongly bundled beam of light. This coat of arms of Australia is a clear example.

PASPOORTEN

Paspoorten is a result of the translation of the book from 1987, which is included in the Best Dutch Book Design

The book starts of with the complete design techniques used back in the 80s. These techniques serve the purpose of showing the authenticity of passports.
